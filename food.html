Foods in Kerala

PUTTU

Puttu is a quintessential breakfast dish from Kerala, celebrated for its simplicity, unique preparation, and cultural significance. This iconic food consists of steamed cylinders of ground rice layered with grated coconut, creating a soft, crumbly texture that is both light and filling. Traditionally, puttu is made using a special vessel called the puttu kudam or puttu kutti, which has two sections: the lower part holds water, while the upper, cylindrical section is filled with alternating layers of rice flour and coconut. The preparation begins with coarse rice flour‚Äîoften labeled as "puttu podi"‚Äîwhich is moistened with water and a pinch of salt until it reaches a sandy, crumbly consistency. The flour is then layered with fresh grated coconut in the puttu maker: coconut first, then rice flour, repeating the sequence, and finishing with coconut on top. The assembled cylinder is then placed over boiling water, and steam cooks the mixture in just a few minutes, resulting in a fragrant, fluffy rice cake. Puttu is highly versatile in terms of accompaniments. The most classic pairing is with kadala curry‚Äîa robust black chickpea curry rich in coconut and spices. Other popular sides include ripe banana, papadum, green gram curry (payaru), or even sweet options like sugar and ghee. In some regions, it is enjoyed with fish or meat curries, making it adaptable to both vegetarian and non-vegetarian preferences. The dish is not only a staple in Kerala households but also a symbol of comfort and nostalgia, often evoking memories of family breakfasts and festive mornings. Its method of steaming makes it a healthy choice, and the use of coconut adds a distinct flavor and aroma unique to Kerala cuisine. Modern variations include using red rice, wheat, or ragi flour, and puttu-specialty restaurants experiment with innovative fillings and sides, reflecting the dish‚Äôs enduring popularity and adaptability.


SADYA

Sadhya is a traditional vegetarian feast originating from Kerala, India, and holds immense cultural significance among Malayalis. It is typically served during major festivals like Onam and Vishu, as well as special occasions such as weddings and temple celebrations. The word Sadhya means "banquet" in Malayalam, and it is renowned for its elaborate spread of dishes, usually served on a fresh banana leaf. A typical Sadhya meal includes anywhere from 20 to 30 dishes, and in some grand versions, such as the Aranmula Valla Sadhya, it can feature over 60 items. The meal is a harmonious blend of flavors‚Äîsweet, sour, spicy, bitter, and salty‚Äîcarefully balanced to create a wholesome and satisfying dining experience. The dishes are mostly mildly spiced and use local, seasonal ingredients native to Kerala, with coconut playing a central role in many recipes, often in the form of coconut oil, coconut milk, or grated coconut. The main staple is plain boiled rice, served alongside a variety of curries and side dishes such as parippu (lentil curry), sambar (vegetable stew with tamarind), rasam (spicy tamarind soup), avial (mixed vegetables with coconut), olan (gourd and coconut curry), and pachadi (curd-based raita). Other important components include pickles, papad, banana chips, ghee, buttermilk, and a selection of payasams (sweet puddings) served as dessert. Sadhya is traditionally eaten sitting cross-legged on the floor, with food served on a banana leaf and eaten with the right hand. Each dish has a designated place on the leaf, and the meal follows a specific order of serving, reflecting deep-rooted customs and respect for the culinary art. The banana leaf is not only eco-friendly but also believed to enhance digestion and flavor. Preparation for Sadhya is meticulous, often starting a day in advance, involving prayers and careful selection of fresh ingredients. It symbolizes unity, prosperity, and the joy of togetherness, making it a feast that is as much about community and culture as it is about food.


UNNIYAPPAM

Unniyappam is a beloved sweet snack from Kerala, South India, known for its unique blend of flavors and cultural significance. The name comes from Malayalam, where unni means "small" and appam means "rice cake," aptly describing these small, round fritters. Traditionally, unniyappam is prepared using a mixture of rice, banana, jaggery, and coconut, along with aromatic spices such as cardamom and sometimes sesame seeds. The preparation begins with soaking short-grained rice, which is then ground with ripe bananas and jaggery to form a semi-fine batter. Chopped coconut pieces are fried in coconut oil or ghee until golden and added to the batter, imparting a delightful crunch and flavor. Some recipes also incorporate wheat flour or rava (semolina) for a different texture, but the classic version remains gluten-free and vegan-friendly when made solely with rice. Spices like cardamom, cumin powder, and dry ginger powder are often included, enhancing the aroma and taste. A pinch of baking soda may be added for a softer texture, though it is optional. The batter is poured into an appe chatti (a special pan with small, round moulds) or an √Übleskiver pan and cooked over low to medium heat until each unniyappam is golden and crisp on the outside, yet soft and spongy inside. Unniyappam holds a special place in Kerala's culinary and religious traditions. It is commonly served during festivals like Onam and is often offered as prasadam (sacred food) in temples. The snack is cherished by Malayalees, much like parathas are loved in Punjab, and is enjoyed warm or at room temperature as a treat for all ages. With its combination of wholesome ingredients and rich cultural roots, unniyappam continues to be a cherished delicacy that brings together tradition, flavor, and nostalgia in every bite.


PALPAYASAM

palpayasam(also spelled Paal Payasam) is a classic and cherished dessert from Kerala, often regarded as the highlight of festive meals and temple offerings in the region. It is a rich, creamy rice pudding made primarily with milk, rice, and sugar, slowly cooked to a luscious consistency that embodies the essence of Kerala's culinary heritage. The traditional recipe uses raw rice (often a variety called unakkalari or chamba rice), which is pressure-cooked or simmered in milk until soft. The milk is typically reduced by slow cooking to intensify the flavor and creaminess. Sugar is added to sweeten the dish, and saffron or cardamom may be used to enhance its aroma and taste. Some versions include nuts like cashews and raisins fried in ghee, which add texture and richness. Palpayasam holds a deep cultural and religious significance in Kerala. It is an essential part of the Onam Sadya feast and other celebrations like Vishu and temple festivals. The dessert is also traditionally served as prasadam (sacred offering) in many temples, symbolizing purity and prosperity. It is often one of the first solid foods given to babies during the Annaprashan ceremony, marking its importance in life rituals. A special variation called Pink Palada Payasam is made using rice ada (rice flakes or rice pasta), milk, and caramelized sugar, which imparts a beautiful pink hue and a distinct caramel flavor. This version requires patience and slow cooking, sometimes taking hours to achieve the perfect creamy texture and color. The preparation of palpayasam demands careful attention to avoid burning the milk and to ensure the rice cooks evenly. The slow simmering process allows the flavors to meld beautifully, resulting in a dessert that is both comforting and indulgent. Its simplicity, combined with the richness of milk and the subtle sweetness, makes palpayasam a timeless favorite across Kerala and beyond.
